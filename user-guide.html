Introduction

DockEase is a browser-based molecular docking platform that puts the power of AutoDock Vina, AutoDock-GPU, Uni-Dock, and Smina in your browser — no installation, no command line, no PhD in bioinformatics required. Upload your receptor and ligands, configure your docking box, and get ranked binding poses with affinity scores in minutes.

Tagline: Upload. Click. Discover.

Whether you are an academic researcher screening a library of 10,000 compounds, a biotech startup validating a lead series, or a student learning structure-based drug design, DockEase scales to your workflow and your budget.

What is Molecular Docking?

Molecular docking is a computational method that predicts the preferred orientation and binding affinity of a small molecule (ligand) inside the active site of a protein (receptor). The output is a ranked list of binding poses, each with a free energy of binding score (kcal/mol). Lower (more negative) scores indicate stronger predicted binding.

Receptor (protein) + Ligand (small molecule) → Predicted binding poses + ΔG scores

DockEase automates the entire pipeline:

mermaidflowchart LR
    A[Upload Receptor] --> B[Auto-Prepare PDBQT]
    C[Upload / Input Ligand] --> D[Auto-Prepare PDBQT]
    B --> E[Detect Binding Site]
    D --> E
    E --> F[Docking Engine]
    F --> G[Ranked Results]
    G --> H[3D Pose Viewer]
    G --> I[ADMET Prediction]
    G --> J[Score Calibration]
    G --> K[Fragment Growing]

Receptors

A receptor is the protein or macromolecular target you wish to dock ligands against. DockEase accepts PDB and PDBQT format files and automatically prepares them for docking.

Uploading a Receptor

  1. Inside your project, click the Receptors tab.
  2. Click + Add Receptor.
  3. Choose Upload File and select your .pdb or .pdbqt file (max 50 MB).
  4. (Optional) Enter a receptor name and notes.
  5. Click Upload.

Figure 4: Uploading a receptor. PDB files are automatically processed through PDBFixer (to repair missing residues and fix non-standard residues) and OpenBabel (to add Gasteiger charges and generate PDBQT).

Fetching from RCSB PDB

If your target structure is in the RCSB Protein Data Bank:

  1. Click + Add Receptor → Fetch from PDB.
  2. Enter the 4-character PDB ID (e.g., 2ITO, 6LU7).
  3. Click Fetch. The structure is downloaded and prepared automatically.

Tip: For co-crystallised structures, DockEase will keep the protein chain only. Crystallographic waters and co-factors are removed during preparation unless you opt to retain them in advanced settings.

Automatic Receptor Preparation

When you upload a PDB file, DockEase runs a preparation pipeline in the background:

PDB file
  └── PDBFixer
        ├── Add missing residues
        ├── Add missing atoms
        ├── Fix non-standard residues
        └── Add hydrogens
  └── OpenBabel
        ├── Assign Gasteiger charges
        └── Generate PDBQT

Preparation typically completes in under 60 seconds. You will see a spinner on the receptor card; once it turns green, the receptor is ready to use.

Binding Site Detection

DockEase offers two methods to define the docking search box:

Automated Detection (P2Rank)

  1. Open the receptor, click Detect Binding Sites.
  2. P2Rank analyses the receptor surface and returns ranked predicted pockets.
  3. Each pocket is shown with a predicted probability score and estimated volume (Å³).
  4. Click Use This Site to apply the site's bounding box to your docking job.

Figure 5: Automated binding site detection with P2Rank. Pockets are ranked by probability. Green = highest confidence; yellow = moderate.

Manual Box Definition

For cases where you know the binding site (e.g., from a co-crystal structure):

  1. Open the receptor's 3D Viewer.
  2. Click Define Box Manually.
  3. Drag the box handles or enter coordinates directly: Center (x, y, z) and Size (x, y, z) in Ångströms.
  4. Click Save Box.

Best practice: Set the box to at least 20 × 20 × 20 Å for a typical pocket. For allosteric or large groove sites, increase to 30 × 30 × 30 Å.

Ligands

A ligand is the small molecule you wish to dock. DockEase supports multiple input methods and prepares ligands automatically.

Upload a File

Accepted formats: SDF, MOL2 (single or multi-structure).

  1. Click the Ligands tab inside your project.
  2. Click + Add Ligand → Upload File.
  3. Select your SDF or MOL2 file. Multi-structure SDF files are split into individual ligand records.
  4. Click Upload.

Enter a SMILES String

  1. Click + Add Ligand → From SMILES.
  2. Paste or type your SMILES (e.g., CC(=O)Oc1ccccc1C(=O)O for aspirin).
  3. A 2D structure depiction is rendered in real time.
  4. Enter a ligand name.
  5. Click Add.

Figure 6: SMILES input with live 2D preview. The depiction updates as you type, giving immediate visual feedback on the structure.

Import from PubChem

  1. Click + Add Ligand → From PubChem.
  2. Enter a CID (e.g., 2244) or a compound name (e.g., "aspirin").
  3. DockEase fetches the canonical SMILES and generates the 3D conformer.
  4. Click Import.

Automatic Ligand Preparation

All ligands go through the following pipeline:

SMILES / SDF / MOL2
  └── RDKit
        ├── Sanitise molecule
        ├── Generate 3D conformer (ETKDG)
        └── Assign partial charges (MMFF94)
  └── Meeko
        └── Generate PDBQT with torsion tree

Ligand preparation is fast (typically under 10 seconds per ligand).

Score Calibration

Industry-First Feature — Unique to DockEase

What is Score Calibration?

Raw docking scores are not inherently calibrated. A score of −8.0 kcal/mol may be excellent for one target and mediocre for another. The Score Calibration feature uses your known actives (compounds you know bind) and inactives (compounds you know do not bind) to compute a target-specific score threshold with statistical rigour.

Under the hood, DockEase:

  1. Docks all your actives and inactives against your receptor
  2. Computes a Receiver Operating Characteristic (ROC) curve — a plot of True Positive Rate vs. False Positive Rate across all possible score thresholds
  3. Calculates the AUC (Area Under the Curve) — a single number from 0.5 (random) to 1.0 (perfect)
  4. Identifies the optimal threshold using Youden's J statistic (J = Sensitivity + Specificity − 1), which maximises the balance between catching true actives and excluding false positives

When to Use Score Calibration

  • You have literature data: IC50 / Ki measurements for your target
  • You are running a prospective screen and want a defensible activity cutoff
  • You want to compare enrichment performance across docking engines for your specific target

Step-by-Step: Running Score Calibration

  1. Open your Receptor and click the Calibrate tab.
  2. Upload or select your known actives (SDF or ligand IDs from your library).
  3. Upload or select your known inactives (decoys — use DUD-E decoys if available).
  4. Click Run Calibration.

Figure 11: Score calibration setup. A minimum of 5 actives and 10 inactives is required. For statistically meaningful results, we recommend at least 20 actives and 50 inactives.

  1. DockEase docks all compounds and generates the ROC curve.

Figure 12: The ROC curve generated after calibration. The AUC is displayed in the legend. The red dot marks the optimal Youden's J threshold — the score cutoff that maximises enrichment for this receptor.

  1. The calibration result displays:
  2. AUC score (0.5–1.0)
  3. Optimal affinity threshold (e.g., −7.8 kcal/mol)
  4. Sensitivity at threshold (% actives correctly identified)
  5. Specificity at threshold (% inactives correctly excluded)
  1. Click Apply to Screen to use this threshold to automatically flag hits in your current virtual screening results.

Interpreting AUC Values

AUCInterpretation
0.5No better than random — docking not predictive for this target
0.6–0.7Below average
0.7–0.8Acceptable enrichment
0.8–0.9Good
0.9–1.0Excellent

Caution: AUC can be inflated if your inactives are not true non-binders (i.e., they have never been tested against your target). Using carefully curated decoy sets (e.g., property-matched DUD-E decoys) gives more reliable calibration.

Fragment Growing

Industry-First Feature — Unique to DockEase

What is Fragment Growing?

Fragment growing (also called fragment elaboration or lead optimisation) is a medicinal chemistry strategy where you take a docked fragment or lead compound and systematically explore chemical space by attaching small fragments at available attachment points. DockEase automates this computationally:

  1. You pick a docked ligand pose and choose an attachment atom on that molecule
  2. DockEase enumerates 24 medicinal chemistry fragments (common R-groups: methyl, ethyl, fluorine, chlorine, hydroxyl, methoxy, trifluoromethyl, amino, cyano, carboxyl, acetamido, morpholino, piperazinyl, pyridyl, phenyl, difluoromethyl, cyclopropyl, sulfamido, methanesulfonyl, and more)
  3. Each variant is prepared and docked in batch
  4. Results are ranked by ΔΔG — the improvement in binding affinity relative to the parent compound

When to Use Fragment Growing

  • You have a validated lead compound and want to improve potency
  • You are in a fragment-based drug discovery (FBDD) campaign
  • You want to explore SAR (Structure-Activity Relationships) computationally before synthesis

Step-by-Step: Running Fragment Growing

  1. In your Results Table, find the pose you wish to grow from and click the Grow button in the action column.

Figure 13: Initiating Fragment Growing from the results table. The Grow button is available for any completed docking result.

  1. The Fragment Growing panel opens, showing the 2D structure of your compound with clickable atoms.
  2. Click on the attachment atom — the atom where you want to add fragments. Eligible atoms are highlighted in green (hydrogens can be substituted; positions with a hydrogen are attachment-eligible).
  3. Review the 24 fragment variants listed below the structure.
  4. Click Grow All to dock all 24 variants, or select specific fragments and click Grow Selected.

Figure 14: The Fragment Growing panel. Green atoms are eligible attachment points. The fragment list shows the chemical structure and SMILES of each fragment to be added.

  1. DockEase creates a batch docking job. Progress is shown in real time.
  1. Results display a ΔΔG column:
ΔΔG = Affinity(variant) − Affinity(parent)

Negative ΔΔG = improved binding (better than parent)
Positive ΔΔG = worse binding

Figure 15: Fragment Growing results ranked by ΔΔG. A fluorine substituent shows the greatest improvement at −1.2 kcal/mol. Click any row to view the pose in the 3D viewer.

  1. Click any variant to view the docked pose alongside the parent compound for direct visual comparison.

Interpreting Fragment Growing Results

  • ΔΔG < −0.5 kcal/mol: Meaningful improvement; worth pursuing experimentally
  • ΔΔG −0.5 to 0: Marginal; may improve other properties (solubility, selectivity)
  • ΔΔG > 0: Fragment reduces predicted affinity at this position

Note: Fragment Growing is a computational shortcut, not a replacement for synthesis. Always validate computationally promising fragments experimentally before committing synthetic resources.

Tips & Troubleshooting

Receptor Preparation Failed

Symptom: Red error badge on receptor card.

Common causes and fixes:

  • Non-standard residues not handled: Try uploading a cleaned PDB with only the protein chain (remove heteroatoms manually in PyMOL or Chimera, then re-upload)
  • File is actually PDBQT: If your file is already a PDBQT, rename it .pdbqt — DockEase will skip preparation and use it directly
  • Very large structure (>5000 residues): Contact support; large multi-chain complexes may require manual preparation

Docking Job Stuck in "Queued"

Cause: High queue load, especially for GPU jobs.

Actions:

  • GPU jobs (AutoDock-GPU, Uni-Dock) share a GPU queue. During peak hours, wait time may be 5–15 minutes.
  • Switch to AutoDock Vina (CPU) for immediate processing if results are urgent.
  • Enterprise customers have a dedicated GPU queue with guaranteed SLA.

Results Look Unreasonable (Very High or Very Low Scores)

Common causes:

  • Box is too large: A 50 Å³ box gives the ligand too much freedom. Shrink to 20–25 Å on each side.
  • Box is misplaced: Verify the box center aligns with your intended binding site in the 3D viewer.
  • Ligand preparation issue: Check that the ligand has correct valences (no disconnected fragments, valid tautomers).
  • Receptor has missing atoms: Run binding site detection again and check the preparation log.

3D Viewer Not Loading

  • Ensure your browser supports WebGL (Chrome/Firefox/Edge on desktop recommended).
  • Safari on iOS has limited WebGL support — use Chrome on mobile for 3D viewing.
  • Clear your browser cache and reload.

WebSocket Disconnected During Job

DockEase uses exponential backoff reconnection. If you see "Reconnecting..." in the job card, this is normal — the connection will re-establish within seconds. Job progress is never lost; it will be synced when reconnected.

FAQ

Q: Can I use DockEase completely free?

A: Yes. The Free tier gives you 5 docking jobs per month with no credit card required. All core features including the 3D viewer and ADMET predictions are available on Free.

Q: How accurate are the docking scores?

A: AutoDock Vina scores correlate with experimental Ki values with R² ≈ 0.5–0.6 on standard benchmarks (CASF-2016). They are best used for rank-ordering compounds within a series, not for absolute binding affinity prediction. Use Score Calibration to establish a target-specific activity threshold.

Q: Is my data private?

A: Yes. Your receptor and ligand files, docking results, and ADMET data are private to your account by default. Files are stored encrypted at rest. We do not use your data to train models or share it with third parties. See our Privacy Policy for full details.

Q: Can I run docking offline?

A: DockEase is a cloud-native platform. For offline/on-premises deployment, contact us about our Enterprise On-Premises edition.

Q: What is the maximum number of ligands I can screen in one job?

A: Free/Starter: 100 ligands per job. Pro: 10,000 per job (Uni-Dock/GPU). Enterprise: 50,000+ per job.

Q: Do you support covalent docking?

A: Covalent docking via Smina with a covalent constraint is on our roadmap for Q3 2026.

Q: How do I cite DockEase in a publication?

A: Please cite: DockEase: Cloud-Based Molecular Docking as a Service (2026). DockEase Inc. https://dockease.io. A formal journal citation is forthcoming.

Q: Can I delete my account and all data?

A: Yes. Go to Account → Settings → Danger Zone → Delete Account. This permanently deletes all your data in accordance with GDPR Article 17 (Right to Erasure) within 30 days.

Q: Is Fragment Growing available on the Free tier?

A: Fragment Growing is available on Starter and above. Each Fragment Growing run consumes jobs from your monthly quota (1 run = up to 24 docking jobs).

Q: Why does score calibration require both actives AND inactives?

A: ROC analysis requires known positives and negatives. Without inactives, it is impossible to measure the false positive rate. Use property-matched decoys (DUD-E or DUDE-Z) for the most rigorous calibration.
